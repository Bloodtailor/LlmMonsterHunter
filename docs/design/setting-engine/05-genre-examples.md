# 05 — Five genres and how they'd actually play

The engine's *true* genre is **"turn-based attrition between characters
who have personas, gathered into a party you grow across risky outings."**
A setting works if it can answer four questions: who's your party, what's
a run, what's a conflict, and what do you lose. Below, five settings that
answer them nothing alike — each with the mechanical mapping and a play
vignette so you can feel it.

For each, the fixed skeleton is identical to fantasy: a 6-rung wellbeing
ladder, two reserve pools, the referee picking an impact word, party
roles (tank/striker/support…), recruitment, memories, affinity, bonds.
Only the *labels and flavor* change.

---

## 1. Highschool Drama — *"Mean Girls with a party system"*

The pure stress-test: combat is entirely **social**, no fists.

- **Party:** classmates you win over — the theatre kid, the burnout, the
  student-council shark. Persona maps perfectly: the *wish* is a crush or
  an ambition, the *secret* is a literal secret.
- **A run:** a charged week — homecoming, exam crunch, the house party.
  Goal: win the election / kill the rumor before it spreads. Stakes:
  your reputation.
- **A conflict:** a showdown in the cafeteria. Turn-based, but "attacks"
  are cutting remarks, cold shoulders, and dropped secrets.
- **Wellbeing ladder:** `composed → rattled → flustered → cornered →
  tearful → humiliated` (bottom rung = flees the scene, the social KO).
- **Reserves:** *Composure* and *Cred* (social capital). Abilities:
  "rally your friends," "expose a text," "play innocent."
- **Danger ladder:** `chill → tense → ruthless`.
- **Recruitment:** befriending — convince a rival to sit at your table.
- **Art:** yearbook-photo / anime.

> *Vignette.* Third period, the cafeteria goes quiet. Brianna has your
> secret and she's smiling. Your Composure is already **rattled**. You
> could deny it (safe, but she presses), or spend your last Cred to flip
> the room — remind everyone what *she* did at the lake house. The
> referee weighs it: **heavy**. Brianna drops to **cornered**. Your
> transfer-student ally, still **wary** of you, decides on her own
> whether to back your play or stay out of it.

---

## 2. Age of Sail — *"recruit a crew, chase a legend across a real ocean"*

Grounded, physical, no magic — an easy fit that proves the engine does
"realistic adventure" fine.

- **Party:** crew — a gunner, a navigator, a cook, a quartermaster with a
  grudge.
- **A run:** a voyage to a marked island or a rumored wreck. Danger:
  storms, reefs, the King's navy.
- **A conflict:** a boarding action, a cannon duel, a tavern brawl.
- **Wellbeing ladder:** `hale → winded → bloodied → reeling → down →
  out cold`.
- **Reserves:** *Vigor* and *Nerve*. Elements → gone; "class" becomes a
  specialty (marksman / powder / blades / sawbones).
- **Recruitment:** press-gang or persuade a marooned sailor to sign the
  articles.
- **Art:** painterly maritime realism.

> *Vignette.* You come alongside a becalmed galleon at dusk. Your goal —
> the strongbox in the captain's cabin — is three decks down. Grapples
> bite. Your bosun (role: *tank*) goes over first to hold the rail; the
> referee spends his **Vigor** as *moderate*. Below, something worse than
> sailors is waiting, and the voyage's danger was posted **perilous** —
> so every blow will land harder than it would on a calm run.

## 3. Deep-Space Salvage — *"Alien, with a party of contractors"*

Sci-fi survival-horror. Same skeleton, dread instead of wonder.

- **Party:** salvage contractors with augments — a breacher, a medic, a
  jittery AI you can carry in a drone body.
- **A run:** dock with a derelict hulk; recover the black box. Danger:
  the thing still aboard, hull breaches, failing life support.
- **A conflict:** a firefight in a zero-g corridor, or a system-failure
  crisis you fight as if it were an enemy.
- **Wellbeing ladder:** `nominal → strained → injured → failing →
  critical → flatlined`.
- **Reserves:** *O₂/Stamina* and *Focus* (nerve under pressure). "Mana"
  → power cells or steady hands.
- **Recruitment:** convince a lone survivor — or the ship's half-dead AI
  — to trust you enough to join.
- **Art:** photoreal grimdark sci-fi.

> *Vignette.* The black box is behind a door that needs two hands and
> thirty seconds. The lights die. Your medic's Focus was already
> **strained**, and now something is moving in the vent. Your goal is
> *right there* — do you burn the AI's last power cell to force the door
> (and go dark), or hold formation? Whatever's coming, the referee is
> about to name how hard it hits.

## 4. Culinary Showdown — *"Iron Chef with a roster and rivalries"*

This one bends "combat" the hardest — which makes it the most fun proof
that the engine reaches past literal fighting (and the clearest place it
strains; see doc 03's reskin/rewrite line).

- **Party:** your kitchen brigade — a fiery grill cook, a precise
  pastry chef, a sous with something to prove.
- **A run:** a tournament arc, or a single brutal dinner service. Goal:
  win the round / survive the rush. Danger = how savage the judges and
  the ticket rail are.
- **A conflict:** a cook-off. Turn-based, but your "attacks" are courses
  and techniques; your "opponent" is the rival chef *and* the clock. The
  referee tastes each plated dish and picks the impact word — how much it
  wowed the table.
- **Wellbeing ladder** (reinterpreted as *your station's control*):
  `dialed-in → messy → scrambling → in-the-weeds → burning → busted`
  (bottom = eliminated).
- **Reserves:** *Stamina* (line energy) and *Inspiration*.
- **Recruitment:** poach a rival's sous-chef between rounds.
- **Art:** warm, glossy food photography.

> *Vignette.* Dessert round, one burner left, ninety seconds. Your pastry
> chef is **scrambling** and the rival across the pass just plated
> something beautiful. You spend your last **Inspiration** on a plated
> risk — a molten thing that either sets or slumps. The referee tastes
> it: **devastating**. The rival's station drops to **in-the-weeds** and
> the judges lean in.

## 5. Neon-Noir Syndicate — *"a crew of specialists, one last job,
everyone's got a secret"*

Crime/heist/detective. Shows the engine flexing between *verbal* and
*physical* conflict in the same setting.

- **Party:** specialists — a grifter, muscle, a burned-out safecracker,
  a driver who talks too much.
- **A run:** a heist or an investigation. Goal: crack the vault / find
  the informant. Danger: the law, the rival family.
- **A conflict:** an interrogation (verbal) *or* a shootout (physical) —
  same turn engine, different paint.
- **Wellbeing ladder:** `cool → sweating → rattled → made → burned →
  down`.
- **Reserves:** *Nerve* and *Leverage*.
- **Recruitment:** the classic "putting the crew together" — talk a
  retired forger back into one last job.
- **Art:** neon-noir photoreal / graphic-novel ink.

> *Vignette.* Interrogation room, one bare bulb. The mark knows where the
> ledger is; your grifter (role: *controller*) works him while your muscle
> just… stands there, breathing. The mark's Nerve slips to **rattled**.
> You could push — spend your **Leverage**, the photo you're not supposed
> to have — but if the read is wrong he lawyers up and the whole run
> collapses. The referee is listening.

---

## The pattern to notice

- **Easy fits** (pirate, sci-fi, noir-shootout): physical conflict
  reskins the ladders one-to-one. Low risk.
- **Seam-stressers** (highschool, culinary, noir-interrogation): the
  conflict is *social or abstract*, which is exactly where a lazy reskin
  would feel wrong and a good lexicon (composure, judges, leverage) makes
  it sing. These are the settings worth prototyping first, because if
  they feel right, everything easier does too.

Every one of these also inherits the roadmap's drama systems for free: a
**nemesis** is a rival captain / the queen bee / a rival chef / a cop who
won't quit; a **bond** is two deckhands or two students who'd take a hit
for each other; a **request** is a crew member wanting to revisit the
island they were marooned on. Same systems, new masks.
